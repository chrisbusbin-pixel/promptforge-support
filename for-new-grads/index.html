The new grad debt picture (typical)

Most US new grads in 2026 face some combination of:

  • Federal student loans ($25K-$50K typical, ~5-8% APR)
  • Private student loans (sometimes — higher APR, often 6-12%)
  • Credit card debt accumulated during school ($1K-$5K typical, 18-25% APR)
  • Maybe a parent-cosigned loan
  • Maybe medical bills from college years
  • Possibly a car loan

Layered together, the math gets confusing. Each loan has its own servicer, APR, payment date, and minimum. Without consolidating into one view, it's almost impossible to think strategically about payoff.

The new grad debt payoff sprint (12-24 months)

Month 1: Get the picture

List every debt. Federal student loans (check StudentAid.gov for federal balances). Private student loans (each servicer separately). Credit cards. Car loan. Anything else. Enter into DebtFree. See your real debt-free date for the first time.

Months 1-3: Attack the credit cards (avalanche)

Credit card APRs (18-25%) dwarf student loan APRs (5-8%). Avalanche means: pay minimums on student loans + every extra dollar at the highest-APR credit card. Don't touch student loans beyond minimums until cards are gone. Most new grads can knock out $3-$5K of credit card debt in 6-9 months on a starting salary.

Months 4-12: Move to private loans, then federal

Once cards are paid off, attack private student loans (typically 8-10% APR). After private, attack federal (6-7% APR) — but consider whether to keep federal loans for forgiveness eligibility (PSLF, IDR) before aggressive payoff.

Year 2+: Steady payoff + start saving

Once high-APR debt is gone, balance becomes: minimum on remaining federal loans, build emergency fund to $3K-$5K, start retirement contributions (especially employer match), then accelerate remaining federal payoff.

The "should I save first or pay debt first" question

The conventional wisdom for new grads:

  1. $1,000 starter emergency fund first — so a small surprise (car repair, medical copay) doesn't put you back into debt
  2. Get the full employer 401(k) match — this is free money, never skip it even while paying down debt
  3. Attack high-APR debt aggressively (anything above 8-10%)
  4. Build emergency fund to 3-6 months while continuing minimum payments on lower-APR debt
  5. Then accelerate remaining debt + increase retirement contributions

This sequence is not financial advice — talk to a fee-only financial advisor for your specific situation. But it's the framework most personal finance experts recommend for typical new grad situations.

Federal student loan considerations

Some federal student loan strategies that DON'T involve aggressive payoff:

  • Income-Driven Repayment (IDR) — payment based on income, with potential forgiveness after 20-25 years
  • Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF) — 10 years of qualifying employment + payments → balance forgiven
  • Teacher Loan Forgiveness — for K-12 teachers in qualifying schools

If you're eligible for one of these programs, aggressive federal loan payoff can actually cost you forgiveness. Research your eligibility before deciding to attack federal loans aggressively. StudentAid.gov has the official details.
