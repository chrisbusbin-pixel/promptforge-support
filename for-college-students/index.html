Use cases specific to college life

The mini-fridge case

Mini-fridges are typically 2-4 cubic feet. Easy to lose track of items pushed to the back. Common waste pattern: forgotten yogurt, expired milk, fuzzy berries. Log every item as you buy it. Get notified 2 days before expiration.

The shared apartment case

"Whose milk is this?" "Is this still good?" Roommates rarely communicate about food. Use FreshTrack with your own labeled items so you know yours are tracked.

The international/travel case

Going home for break? Open FreshTrack and see what needs to be eaten or given away before you leave. Don't return to a science experiment in the fridge.

The "I'm cooking for one" case

Half a head of lettuce, half a loaf of bread, half a tomato — single-serving cooking creates lots of "almost finished" items. Log them. Plan to use them this week. The notifications keep them top of mind.

The shopping habits that work for college students

Buy frozen produce

Frozen vegetables don't expire. Same nutrition, half the cost, none of the wilting. Especially good for students cooking for one.

Buy smaller portions

Buying the half-loaf of bread is more expensive per ounce but cheaper if the full loaf goes moldy. The cheapest groceries are the ones you actually eat.

Cook in 2-portion batches

Make tonight's dinner + tomorrow's lunch in one cooking session. Better economics than single-portion cooking, less waste than 4-portion.

Use the freezer aggressively

Bread, herbs, cheese, leftover rice, ripe bananas — all freeze well. The freezer is your "save" button when you can't finish something fresh. More on freezing →

Designate an "eat me first" shelf

One area of your fridge is for items expiring this week. Check it every time you open the door. Fridge organization guide →
